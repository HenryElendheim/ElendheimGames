# Design — how the learning is woven into play

## The "natural but fun" ladder
Good games already teach you their own vocabulary by making you *need* it. We
borrow that so Norwegian feels acquired, not studied:

1. **See it before being tested on it.** World objects wear their Norwegian name
   (a cup says *kopp*); tap to hear it. Pure exposure, no quiz.
2. **Need it to do the thing.** The baker says *"to brød, takk"* — the fun is
   *assembling two loaves* because you understood. Comprehension = progress.
3. **See it constantly through play, not drilling.** If your morning routine is
   making *kaffe*, you read *kaffe* 40x/week with zero flashcards. The core trick.
4. **Soft mistakes.** Wrong = NPC tilts head, repeats slower. No buzzer/red X.
   Sometimes the wrong answer is funnier — let her enjoy it.
5. **Scaffolding fades.** Wk1: Norwegian + English hint + picture. Wk4: Norwegian
   + picture. Wk8: just Norwegian. She won't notice the fade — that's the magic.
6. **Hear everything.** `nb-NO` TTS on every word; reading + pronunciation grow
   together.

## NPCs as vocab "worlds"
Befriend townsfolk; each is a domain:
- **Café owner** → food, drink, numbers, money.
- **Fisherman** → weather, sea, nature.
- **Mechanic** → cars, tools, directions (also the gateway to the driving game).
- **Grandma at the pub** → teaches a Norwegian card game (numbers, suits, banter).

## Mechanic ideas to sprinkle (or spin off)
- **"Read the room" dialogue** — decode what an NPC wants from tone + context.
- **Cooking from a Norwegian recipe** — follow steps; good meal vs funny disaster.
- **Decorating your home** — shop furniture described in Norwegian (adjectives,
  colors, rooms).
- **A pet** you name and command (sit/come/eat) — animals + imperatives.
- **Letters from "home"** — short notes that grow longer/harder; warm reason to read.
- **Texting minigame** — chat with an NPC on an in-game phone; casual/slang register.

## Soft structure for an open world
Keep it open but not aimless: invitations, not demands. A neighbor *mentions* the
Saturday market; a "things I could do today" note on the fridge she can ignore;
seasonal events that happen with or without her. Always a thread to pull, never a
checklist staring at her.
